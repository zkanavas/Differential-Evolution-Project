Initial Condition Sensitivity

This project is searching for a quantifiable relationship between pore network structure and velocity distributions. With a known percolating pathway, the project compares the shortest path, using Dijkstra’s algorithm (weighted by various structural attributes), to determine the level of overlap or accuracy . If a path is highly accurate then the weighting scheme used to produce it is said to be influential on the development of preferential flow pathways. The structural attributes explored include:

To search through what (linear) combinations of these structural attributes produces an accurate shortest path, this project employed differential evolution – a genetic algorithm. This algorithm will continuously change the weight multiplied against the structural attribute value to make it more or less impactful to the overall weighting scheme, comparing each population member to find the “fittest” or “most accurate” weighting scheme.

The purpose of this sensitivity analysis is to understand how the results of the differential evolution algorithm respond to different provided initial conditions. A total of 9 different initial conditions were provided to the program:

LatinhybercubeRandom Educated Guess
Uniformly Distributed in BoundsUniform – Long & Thick Attribute EmphasizedDistributed Representation – All
Distributed Representation – Not included: Min/Mean Thickness & Volume AttributesDistributed Representation – Not included: Min/Mean Thickness, Volume, & Poiseuille AttributesDistributed Representation – Reduced

Each set of initial conditions is explained further in subsequent sections, however it should be noted that “latinhybercube” and “random” are population initialization arguments whereas the rest of the initial conditions are user-defined.

This project uses the SciPy function: optimize.differential_evolution. Population initialization using this function requires either a function argument (latinhybercube or random) or an array specifying the initial population. If providing an array, there must be at least 5 vectors. This is because the some of the available mutation strategies require 5 vectors (the best current solution + 4 random population vectors) to create a mutated vector.

The seed was kept constant at 1 to ensure experiment reproducibility; each structural attribute weight was bounded between 0 and 10; the mutation constant was the tuple (0.5,1) which employs dithering (dithering randomly changes the mutation constant on a generation by generation basis); the recombination constant was 0.7; the strategy was "best1bin’" meaning the best current solution vector and 2 random population vectors are used for creating a mutated vector; the absolute tolerance was set to 0 and the relative tolerance was set to 0.01; and polish was set to true meaning scipy.optimize.minimize is used to polish the best population member at the end, which can improve the minimization slightly. With those function input parameters set, the initial population was set by one of the nine options for each experiment to compare the accuracy, runtime, and weights assigned to each structural parameter, as shown in Tables 1 & 2.

Table 1 shows the weight assigned to each structural attribute for a given initial condition; the same information is shown graphically in Figure 1. Immediately, one will notice the high number of attributes incorporated for the Latinhypercube, Random, Educated Guess, and Uniformly Distributed in Bounds initial condition sets. Latinhybercube and the Uniformly Distributed ICs are similar in that the former tries to maximize coverage of the available parameter space and the latter was set up so that each structural attribute weight would be initially spread out between the bounds; due to the nature of these two, it is understandable how the final solution involved so many attributes. Random begins by selecting a vector randomly within the given bounds, then searching until convergence. Latinhybercube, Uniform Distribution & Random ICs experienced early convergence as they did not reach the maximum possible average accuracy (each had 70.4, 65.5, and 70.4%, respectively). Although the Educated Guess reached the maximum accuracy, it did converge with 9 out of the 15 attributes in its solution. This may be due to the second vector in the IC set, it has a value for every structural attribute, thus if that is the best fit solution at any point, the algorithm will attempt to mutate with that vector as opposed to one with fewer attributes. Due to the large number of attributes in the final solution vectors for the mentioned IC strategies, they are not ideal the ideal initial population.

Table 2 shows the respective accuracy for each level of saturation, the mean accuracy, and the program runtime (time to convergence) in seconds.

The Uniform – Long & Thick Attribute Emphasized IC performed quite well in terms of accuracy and had the shortest runtime. However, as seen in Figure 1, the only attribute incorporated was Long & Thick. The latter was caused by the initial condition vectors provided, they were all the same with a 1 assigned to LT; therefore, when attempting to mutate this vector, the program would produce a mutated vector identical to the ICs. The Uniform – LT Emphasized IC is not a viable option for the ICs for this project. The Distributed Representation – All IC yielded similar results, the accuracy was high but its final solution vector only incorporated LT; thus, this IC strategy will not be used for this program.

There are three more IC strategies left to discuss, all are variations of the Distributed Representation – All IC, but exclude certain vectors. Distributed Representation - No Min/Mean Thickness & Volume excluded the minimum thickness, mean thickness, and volume attribute from the initial condition. Please note that this does not mean that these attributes were removed from the program to consider, rather the initial population given no longer highlighted these attributes. This IC strategy produced a solution with LT, ET, AR, and node degree and had a slightly higher accuracy, but interestingly took slightly longer compared to Distributed Representation – All.

The final two IC strategies: Distributed Representation - No Min/Mean Thickness Poiseuille & Volume and Distributed Representation – Reduced produced quite similar final solutions and the Reduced version took half the time as the former. Note, when providing a smaller initial population, the runtime is expected to decrease by nature of the algorithm. However, as the two strategies produced similar final solutions, it would be advantageous to use the shorter algorithm for larger sample sizes.

When comparing the results of the final three strategies, the Distributed Representation – No Min/Mean Thickness, Volume seems the most promising. It is concerning that the other two strategies incorporated tortuosity into their final solution; Tortuosity being a measure of a curve being tortuous (having many turns), a path with higher tortuosity should be more resistant to flow, thus would not allow for a percolating pathway to develop there. On the contrary, these are unsaturated samples, where large pores drain first and smaller pores retain water longer, thus there may be a higher amount of tortuous pathways present in these samples, making them the only option for the flow to propagate through.
